Lesson 71: Japanese counters: 3 simple rules make them easy!

こんにちは.

Today we're going to talk about counters in Japanese.

Now, as we all know, the textbooks like to present Japanese as a kind of mysterious language full of completely random and irrational rules and items that we just have to learn because there's no particular reason for them.

And, as you all know if you've been following my video lessons, most of the time this isn't true.

Most of what goes on in Japanese is completely reasonable and logical if you just know how it works, which the people who write textbooks don't appear to.

However, counters might seem like an exception to this rule.

There are a lot of them.

An awful lot of counters.

In Japanese you can't just say "one dog"or "two flowers"or "three birds".

You have to use a counter.

So we say "ippiki の inu" -- "one dog"-- and the counter here is " hiki", which gets pronounced "piki"in this particular instance.

For "three birds" we say "san は の tori", and the "wa"is a counter.

So it looks as if we have to learn an awful lot of counters, doesn't it, just to say what in English you can say by the number and the thing.

And there really are a lot of counters -- dozens and dozens of them.

So this looks like a problem, doesn't it? But it's not, in fact, nearly as much of a problem as it appears to be.

First of all, although there are an awful lot of counters, only about a dozen of them are really commonly used.

Secondly, most of the time we can avoid them by using the native Japanese counting system, which doesn't require counters.

So if we use the " 一つ, futatsu, mittsu, yottsu, itsutsu"counting system, we don't need any counters.

We can use them for nearly everything -- not people, not small animals, but practically everything else we can use the Japanese counting system if we want to.

So the important thing here, as I'm sure you won't be surprised to hear me say, is that what we're not going to do is start learning lists of counters.

That, if you'll excuse the expression, would simply be counterproductive.

And there are several reasons why we don't need to.

First of all, in terms of understanding Japanese, they don't present many problems at all.

If we hear someone saying a number and a thing we know, if there's a little word in between the two, we know it's a counter.

So we know what they've said.

If they say "ichi rin の hana", we know they're saying "one flower".

"ichi...

hana", and the "rin"must be the counter.

If we hear someone saying "ni は の tori", we know that they're saying "two birds": "ni...

tori", and the "wa"is the counter.

If we hear someone saying "san biki の 猫"-- now, even if we knew " hiki", but we didn't know that at three it becomes "biki", that's not a problem because we know "san", we know "猫", and we know that things that come between numbers and their object are going to be counters.

Should we put them into our Anki? Yes, we should.

We'll get to know them over time.

But we don't have to fret about learning them.

And as I've indicated, the counters aren't pronounced the same way all the time, and that's much less of a problem than it appears to be if we understand how it works.

And I'm going to talk about that towards the end, but it's not really terribly important.

It's actually much more important to understand how they work structurally.

There are two ways of using counters, and it's the second of those ways that I think often confuses foreigners.

The first way, actually, is quite similar to English.

So when we use " hiki"for animals or "mai"for sheets of paper, shall we say, it's not that dissimilar to saying "five head of cattle"or "a dozen sheets of paper"in English.

Not quite the same thing, but it's very similar.

The usage that confuses people is when we say things like "猫が go hiki iます".

And that would be translated as "there are five cats", and that is how we'd say it in English.

In Japanese we're saying something a little different.

And to understand the structure, we need to understand that.

So, first of all, what kind of a word is a counter? What part of speech does it represent? Well, it's not a verb and it's not an adjective.

It's not a particle and it's not a conjunction.

So, nine times out of ten, what is a word if it's not a verb or an adjective or a particle or a conjunction? What is it? It's a noun! Nearly always it's a noun, and that is true of counters.

Counters are nouns.

That's why we use の after them.

We say "ichi rin の hana", "san biki の 猫", just as we would with any other noun: "mahou の tsue" -- "magic wand" ; "san biki の 猫" -- "three cats".

But when we say "猫が san biki いる" -- "there are three cats"in English -- what are we actually saying? Well, the thing to understand here is that counters aren't ordinary nouns.

They are what I called in my video about special noun types, they're what I called "foxy nouns", that is to say, adverbial nouns.

They are nouns that have the special property of being able to modify verbs.

And that's what's happening here.

When we say "猫が san biki iます", we're actually saying "cats, three-small-animals-ly exist".

Now, that's very difficult in English, isn't it? We could make it sound a little bit simpler by saying "cats three-ly exist"or "cats exist threefold-ly", but in fact because of the counter system we're saying "cats exist three-small-animals-ly".

We're describing the way they exist.

They exist in a three-small-animals way.

If there were four of them, they'd be existing in a four-small-animals way.

And that's why sentences like that have to end in a verb.

The verb modified by a counter is often, as in this case, the verb of being, but it doesn't have to be.

For example, we might say "shashin を san mai totta" -- "I took photographs three-flat-things-ly".

And while that's very different from the way we put it in English, it's useful and important for the structure to understand that that is in fact what's happening in the Japanese.

Now, what about learning the counters and learning the sound-changes of the counters, which really tend to worry people.

Now, as I said, we don't need to learn lists of counters.

We'll pick up counters as we go along in our Japanese immersion.

When we encounter a new counter in our reading or our Japaneseすbtitled anime, we can put it into our Anki and we'll gradually build up our stock of counters.

The counters that are commonly used are not that huge in number.

We have "mai"for flat things like sheets of paper or coins.

We have " hon"for long round things, "satsu"for books.

That might seem strange because why do we have "satsu"for books and " hon"for long round things, when " hon" actually means "book"? Well, that's because when the word " hon"first came into being and meant, among other things, a book, at that time books were long round things.

They were scrolls.

So a book was counted in the way that other long round things were counted.

But later on, when books became flat things with pages, the counter was changed to "satsu".

So, we probably need a dozen or so counters for everyday use and we'll pick those up reasonably quickly.

But what about all the sound changes? Let's take " hiki", which is the counter for small animals.

We say "ippiki の 猫"-- one cat, "ni hiki の 猫"-- two cats; "san biki の 猫"-- three cats; "yon hiki の 猫"-- four cats; "go hiki の 猫"-- five cats; "roppiki の 猫"-- six cats; "nana hiki の 猫"-- seven cats; " happiki の 猫 -- eight cats; "急 hiki の 猫"-- nine cats; "juppiki の 猫"-- ten cats.

And then other counters have other variations.

So it starts to seem very, very complicated, but as a matter of fact there aren't that many variations, and they follow a very simple pattern.

First of all, if the first kana of a counter can't take a 〃, then there aren't going to be any variations at all.

That deals with over half the problem straight away.

So, if we have the counters like "mai", counter for flat things like coins or pieces of paper.

Can you put a 〃 on "mai"? No, you can't.

You can't put a 〃 on any of the ma-miむ-me-mo column, and therefore there can't be any variant on "mai": "ichi mai, ni mai, san mai, go mai, yon mai" and so on.

"Rin", the counter for flowers and wheels -- Can you put a 〃 on "ri"? No, you can't.

You can't put a 〃 on any of the ra-ri る -re-ro column, can you? So we know that "rin"can't have any exceptions: "ichi rin, ni rin, san rin, yon rin"etc.

Or how about "wa", the counter for birds? Can you put a 〃 on "wa"? No, you can't.

So again that has to be completely regular: "ichi wa, ni wa, san wa, yon wa..."So now we've disposed of an awful lot of counters, haven't we? All of those are just straightforward and regular.

What about the others? Well, which are the kana onto which you can put a 〃? It's the ta column, the sa column, the ka column and of course the ha column, which can have a 〃 to become ba-bi-bu-be-bo or a maru to become pa-pi-pu-pe-po.

What do we know about these? Well, first of all, we know that in the first and last numbers of the sequence all of them work differently from the nonてnてnable kana and they all work the same as each other.

So if we take "買い" , the counter for floors in a building, we don't say "ichi kai"-- we say "ikkai".

If we take "sai", the counter for years of age, we don't say "ichi sai"-- we say "issai".

If we take " hiki", which we've been dealing with before, we don't say "ichi hiki"-- we say "ippiki".

So that works the same.

And with ten, at the other end of the basic scale, we don't say "juu sai"-- we say "jussai".

We say "jukkai", tenth floor; "juppiki の 猫", ten cats.

Now, the "ka" and " ha"rows, we tend to do the same thing with "roku" and " hachi" as well.

So we say "roppiki; happiki; rokkai; hakkai".

And when we know that we pretty much know all of it.

Sometimes a 〃 goes onto the counter on "san" as well.

So we say "san biki" and we say "sangai".

But this isn't completely regular.

Not everybody says "sangai".

And a lot of the variations aren't completely regular.

The ones I've just told you are pretty regular.

The other odd ones don't tend to be very regular.

So, if you understand that, it sounds a bit complicated.

I wouldn't spend too much time trying to learn what I've just said, but bear it in mind, because it'll just make it a little easier to pick this up when you see it.

"Ippiki, ni hiki, san biki"-- which is a tendency that "san" has, of course only with 〃able kana -- "go hiki"-- that's always going to be the same -- "roppiki""nana hiki"-- that's always the same with everything -- " happiki, 急 hiki, juppiki".

Perhaps even at this stage that's starting to make sense.

It's conforming to a regular pattern.

They aren't all over the place.

There are a few exceptions and nearly all the things that are genuinely exceptional from the pattern I've taught you tend to be optional.

Not all Japanese speakers are going to use them all the time anyway.

They will say "ippiki"; they won't say "ichi hiki"; they will say "roppiki"; they won't say "roku hiki".

If you get it wrong and say "roku hiki", it's really not the end of the world.

Everyone's going to know what you mean.

It'll just sound a little bit cute and foreign.

And you will pick it up over time, because this is the sort of thing you hear again and again, and it just becomes natural.

And because the patterns are almost regular across the range of 〃 kana, you start to know what sounds right, so that without a lot of intensive learning effort you'll be saying it right nine times out of ten after a reasonable amount of exposure.

So it's really not something you need to sit down and learn.

You can save your bandwidth for things that really matter.

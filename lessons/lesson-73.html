Lesson 73: Secrets of 気 Ki - ki になる, ki にする, kiが する, ki ni いる etc. 気になる、気にする、気がする、気に入る

こんにちは.

Today we're going to talk about the word "ki" and various expressions that incorporate it, like "kiに する", "kiが する", "kiに なる", "kiに いる" and so forth.

Now, people often find these confusing and difficult to work out exactly how it is that they're structured, how it is that they work, how we should use them.

And there are two reasons for this.

The first is that we need to understand what "ki"really means.

And the second is that we need a very firm grip on the logical particles if we're going to differentiate things like "kiに する", "kiに なる", "kiが する", from each other.

And of course if you learn conventional textbook Japanese, a firm grasp on the unchanging nature of the logical particles is exactly what you don't have, because if you are taught things like "コーヒーが 好き だ" means "I like coffee", you never really understand what the unchanging nature of the が particle, for example, is.

And if you do have any doubts about the nature of the five main logical particles, I do recommend that you watch this video on the subject, because it is the very foundation of Japanese.

If you're fuzzy about the logical particles, then you're fuzzy about Japanese, period.

All right.

So what is "ki"? "Ki"comes from Chinese.

The kanji is the same kanji that we have in "tai chi".

It's a Daoist concept and probably goes back to a time before what we now know as Daoism.

To this day the concept is used in Chinese medicine, Chinese and Japanese martial arts, and other areas.

What it is is the fundamental spirit of fluid energy in a person or something else.

Originally it was associated with breath, and in many languages, "breath" and "spirit" are in fact the same word.

Latin "spiritus", Greek "pneuma", Hebrew "ruach", Sanskrit "atma": all mean both breath and spirit.

Now, the breath meaning of "ki"is no longer active, but it's still this flowing spirit principle within a person.

If we look at the kanji, interestingly, this part of it (which is also pronounced "ki") means steam or vapor.

So, a "ki-sha"is a steam engine.

And then we have this "x", as it were, and here "x"really "marks the spot".

We have the fixed cross portion and we have the fluid, vaporous portion.

And both of those aspects of "ki" are important, and you can never entirely separate the two, although we can move from the fixed end of the scale to the fluid end of the scale.

So, to give examples of those two ends of the scale, let's take two expressions that you almost certainly know.

The first is "genki", the second is " や ruki".

Now, "genki", as you know, is vigor or health.

The "gen" means original, fundamental.

So, one's original "ki"is healthy and vigorous.

If something goes wrong, we may become "byouki" (sick or dispirited).

And our aim is then to return our "ki" to its original healthy and vigorous state, to restore our "genki".

" や ruki"is the will to do something.

So it's the do-spirit, get-up-and-go-spirit.

And this kind of "ki"is not our basic "ki" that in a sense remains constant within us.

It's a particular "ki", the "ki" to do a particular thing.

So we might say "kaigai de 勉強 する kiが ある".

And what we're saying here is that a particular "ki"exists in us that is the "ki" to study abroad.

So in this sense "ki"is the will or inclination to do a particular thing.

When we say "ki を tsukeru", this literally means attach or stick your spirit.

So if someone said "kaidanに ki を tsukeru", what they're literally saying is "attach your spirit to the staircase".

Now, what we'd say in English is "watch out for the staircase"or "take care on the staircase".

Attach your spirit, your consciousness, your feelings, your thoughts, to the staircase; pay attention to it; take care.

And it may not be a particular thing.

Very often people say "ki を tsukete" (attach your spirit).

And this is not attach it to any particular thing, but attach it to whatever it's appropriate to attach it to; have situational awareness, as one might say in a more modern jargon; take care.

Now, when we get on to things like "kiに なる" and "kiに する"-- we've dealt in a previous lesson with "に なる" and "に する".

So, for example, if we say "さくらが kaeruに なる", we're saying Sakura turns into a frog.

If we say "majoが Sakura を kaeruに する", we're saying that a witch turns Sakura into a frog.

And of course we know who is doing what because of the logical particles.

So what does it mean when we talk about "kiに なる" and "kiに する"? We're saying that something becomes our spirit.

What would we mean by that? Well, to understand that we need to understand something else.

We need to understand that becoming a feeling, an emotion, the expression of emotion, is something that we do in Japanese.

We don't do it in English.

So, for example, in AKB48's song " heavy Rotation" we have the line: "こんな kimochiに narerutte bokuは tsuite いる ne".

And this means "that I could become this feeling, I'm lucky, aren't I?"Now, in English we don't talk about "becoming" a feeling, but in Japanese we do talk about that.

And I think this is somewhat connected with the concept of "ki" and also with the Buddhist idea that there really is no self.

What we are is not a fixed and absolute thing.

The self is essentially a shifting congeries of actions and reactions, feelings and sensations.

And so we may temporarily become a particular feeling.

You may even become, in Japanese, the expression of a feeling.

We may say "shinkoku-na houjouに なる"-- someone became a grave or concerned face.

For that moment, the whole person transformed into that expression of gravity and concern.

Now, this is what we're saying when we say "kiに なる" and "kiに する".

If we say "ano hitoは kiに なる", we're saying that person turns into my spirit.

My spirit is remolded, as it were, a certain part of it, for a certain period, into the form or shape of that person.

What does that mean? It means that that person is, as we would say in English, occupying my feelings.

Now, this can mean a variety of things.

It can mean that we're concerned or worried about that person.

It can mean that that person intrigues us.

It can mean that we feel there's some mystery about that person.

Or whatever it is, that person is occupying our feelings, our sensations, our thoughts; in Japanese, is becoming our feelings, our sensations, our thoughts, our "ki".

Now, an important thing to notice here is that we tend to see in grammar books and dictionaries "'kiに なる' means this", "'kiに する' means that", but the important thing to understand is that "kiに なる"on its own doesn't mean anything.

Neither does "kiに する".

"Kiに なる" is the tail-end of a complete logical clause.

"なる" is the engine, "kiに"is telling us more about the engine.

The A-car in the case of "kiに なる" is whatever it is that's occupying our thoughts, whatever it is that's turning into our spirit.

So in this case "ano hito"is the A-car and what we're saying about that person, what that person is doing, is turning into our spirit.

Now, when we take "kiに する", we say "ano hito を kiに する".

So now the doer of the action is not that person; the doer of the action is me.

I am turning that person into my spirit.

I am being concerned about that person.

And that has two implications.

One is that in a sense we're doing violence to our spirit.

It's not just our spirit naturally becoming that person -- we are making it so.

And that tends to give it a slightly more negative meaning.

While "kiに なる"can occupy a whole spectrum of meaning from being intrigued to being worried, "kiに する"usually means worried.

Now, the other implication it has is that we're in control of it.

Our spirit isn't simply turning into that person.

We are turning our spirit into that person.

And that's why you often hear people say "kiに shiないで" (don't turn it into your spirit).

It wouldn't make any sense to say "kiに naraないで", because you aren't making anything happen.

Your spirit's simply turning into it.

But "kiに shiないで"is telling you to control something that you have control over: "Don't turn it into your spirit; don't worry about it; don't be concerned."Now, we have "kiに する" and "kiに なる".

What about "kiが する"? Again, the particle gives us the clue to what's going on.

When we say "kiが する", it's the "ki", the spirit itself, that's acting.

It's not something becoming our spirit, it's not us turning something into our spirit.

It's our spirit doing something or a certain aspect of our spirit acting ("する", do or act).

How do we understand this? Well, again we need to understand something else in Japanese before we can properly understand this.

When we use expressions like "ii nioiが する"or "きれい-na otoが する", what we're saying is "there's a good smell", "there's a pretty sound/ a pretty sound happened/ a pretty sound acted".

What we mean by "する" in the case of a smell or a sound is the only thing a smell or a sound can do: a smell smells and a sound sounds.

When we say "nioiが する", we're saying that a smell acts, a smell is active, I can detect a smell.

"Otoが する": a sound acts, a sound is active, I can detect a sound.

When we say "kiが する", we're saying a certain spirit, a certain thought, a certain consciousness is active, I can detect that consciousness, I can detect that feeling.

So, if we say "damasarete いる you-na kiが する", we're saying "I've got a feeling we're being deceived, I've got a feeling we're being tricked"-- such a "ki", such a feeling, is active.

If we say "tsuyokuなった kiが する", we're saying "I feel that I got stronger, I sense that I got stronger"-- that spirit, that inclination of the emotions, of the thoughts, is active in the same way that a smell is active or a noise is active.

Now, one last one that I think is more confusing than the others because essentially it uses a word in an uncommon way is "kiに いる".

If we say "sore を kiに いる", we're saying "I like that".

Now, what we're really saying is that I'm letting that into my soul.

What's confusing here is that "いる" is not being used in its normal way.

"いる"usually means to enter or come in.

And if we're talking about putting something into something, we say "ireru".

But in this case, "いる", probably because it's an older usage that's cemented into the language in this particular very common phrase, "いる" actually means allow into one's spirit.

So, the one who is doing "kiに いる" is oneself.

And we know that is the case because that's the way the particles are working.

We can always trust the particles.

If the particles are saying one thing and the form of the word is saying another, we can be confident in every case that the particles know what they're talking about and the word may have changed its meaning over time.

"Kiに いる" is something you'll hear on various occasions, and a particular characteristic of this is that it's much easier to use it about other people than things like "ほしい" or "好き".

This is not to say that we can baldly state what other people like and what they don't like, even using "kiに いる", but if we're going to talk about other people's feelings, ask them about their feelings, suggest what their feelings might be, "kiに いる" is much politer than using terms like "たい" or "好き"or "ほしい".

So you'll very often see "kiに いる" in references to other people's feelings as well as to one's own.

So, I hope this makes the whole "ki"usage a little clearer.
